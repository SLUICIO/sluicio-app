# Personal LinkedIn post — "Monitoring should save money, not cost money"

**Pillar:** Positioning & Cost (personal/opinion) · **Channel:** LinkedIn (personal profile)
**Angle:** Problem-first opinion. Monitoring is sold as insurance but priced like a luxury; it should pay for itself fast.

---

## Version A — recommended

Somewhere along the way, monitoring got expensive to *have* and expensive to *set up*. That's backwards.

Monitoring is supposed to save you money. Catch the outage before the customer does. Find the slow hop before it becomes a refund. Shorten the 2 a.m. guessing game from three hours to ten minutes.

Instead, too many teams I talk to are stuck on the other side of the ledger:

– A six-figure bill that grows with every new service, so they sample away the data they'll wish they had during the next incident.
– A multi-week rollout with a consultant, because "getting started" means a project, not an afternoon.
– A tool so complex that two people understand it and everyone else opens a ticket.

If your monitoring costs more than the downtime it prevents, it's not insurance. It's overhead with a dashboard.

I think the bar should be simple:

→ **Fast to set up** — point a collector at it, see data the same day. Not next quarter.
→ **Valuable immediately** — the first trace should already answer a question you couldn't answer yesterday.
→ **Easy to live with** — if you need a certification to read your own telemetry, something's wrong.

That's the standard I'm holding Sluicio to. Monitoring should land on the *savings* side of the ledger — from day one.

Curious what others think: what's the most you've ever paid for monitoring that didn't actually save you anything? 👇
